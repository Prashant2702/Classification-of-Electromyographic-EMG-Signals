What are EMG Signals?

Electromyographic (EMG) signals are the electrical signals generated by skeletal muscles when they contract in response to signals from the nervous system. These signals are a result of the electrical activity produced by muscle fibers when stimulated by motor neurons. EMG signals are widely used in medical and research fields to analyze muscle function, detect abnormalities, and assist in the development of human-machine interaction systems. The study of these signals is known as electromyography.

EMG Signal Capture Techniques

EMG signals can be captured through two main techniques:

  • Surface EMG (sEMG): Non-invasive, using electrodes placed on the skin above the muscle, commonly used in sports science, rehabilitation, and gesture recognition.
  • Intramuscular EMG: Invasive, requiring needle electrodes inserted into the muscle, mainly used in clinical diagnosis for assessing deeper or smaller muscles.

Signal Characteristics

The characteristics of EMG signals include:

  • Amplitude ranges from 50 microvolts to several millivolts
  • Frequency ranges from 10 Hz to 500 Hz
  • Signals are often noisy and non-stationary

Signal preprocessing such as filtering, rectification, and normalization is essential before analysis.

Applications

EMG signals have a broad range of applications, including:

  • Diagnosing neuromuscular disorders (muscular dystrophy, ALS)
  • Controlling prosthetic limbs
  • Aiding in physical therapy
  • Enhancing sports performance monitoring
  • Machine learning applications for movement prediction
